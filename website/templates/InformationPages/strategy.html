Basic Strategy



Before talking about counting cards, basic strategy needs to be mentioned. Basic strategy is the set of all actions players should make given any hand in Blackjack. If a player plays Blackjack according to basic strategy, then that player will still be at a disadvantage to the house, but only slightly. Following perfect basic strategy is not enough to become profitable by itself; players also need to count cards and play with proper bet spreads to make a profit in the long run. However, the current focus is on basic strategy for now.


The following image (Figure 48) is an example of a basic strategy chart. Many of these charts exist online, and they all contain information dependent on the rules of the game played. Many casinos will allow players to bring these charts to the Blackjack table to reference them while playing because even if players follow basic strategy, the casinos are still making money in the long run.


This specific chart is from wizardofodds.com depicting optimal basic strategy play corresponding to Liberal Vegas Strip rules.


Figure 1: Basic strategy chart for “Liberal Vegas rules”. [1]


In order to follow the chart, three types of Blackjack hands need to be introduced: hard, soft, and pairs. Most hands in Blackjack fall under the hard category. A hard hand is simply a hand which neither has an Ace or a pair. A soft hand is any hand containing an Ace (excluding a pair of Aces). These are called soft hands because the Ace can take on either the value of an 11 or a 1. Pairs are what they sound like, they are hands containing two of the same card. In Blackjack, players are allowed the unique option of “splitting” a pair. To do this, a player must match their original bet, and they will now play two hands. Each of the split cards will receive a new card, and each of the two hands are played like normal hands. You can even split again if you make another pair. However, most casinos only allow a max of 3 splits, and split Aces are usually only given 1 extra card thereby ending the player’s turn.


The five options players can make are: hit, stand, double down, split, or surrender. To follow basic strategy, a player determines the type of hand they have, adds up the value of that hand, (assuming it’s not a pair) and compares that value to the card the dealer is showing. Ideally, card counters have this chart memorized, but for the sake of learning, the user will need to look at the table to know which action to take at any given time.


To become an advantaged player, a card counter must perfect basic strategy. Basic strategy will change depending on the game, so players must also know which strategy corresponds to the game they play. The chart discussed in Figure 48 is the basic strategy for a deep deck game where the dealer must stand on a soft 17. Below is the basic strategy where the dealer must hit on a soft 17.


Figure 2: Basic strategy where the dealer must hit on a soft 17. [1]


The difference between Figure 1 and Figure 2 is small, but it’s important. Since the edge the player can have against the house is small, every detail matters.
