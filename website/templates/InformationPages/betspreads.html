Bet Spreads



The idea of a bet spread is to maximize profit while minimizing the risk of going bankrupt. Even if the player is counting cards perfectly, and they’ve done everything discussed so far without fail, they can still lose their entire bankroll if they don’t use a safe bet spread. Winning money by counting cards is a marathon; not a sprint. The profit margins for advantaged players are generally between 1-2%, so variance can play a huge role in profit loss in the short-term. In this section, the idea of the Kelly bet will be introduced.


As it turns out, the idea behind bet spreads should be thought more of in terms of financial investment. However, unlike financial investment, where market returns can fluctuate from unknown variables, the return advantaged players get from Blackjack is exact. Card counting in Blackjack is the perfect example of a guaranteed real-life application of Kelly criterion betting.


The Kelly criterion is a form of scientific gambling where the risk of going bankrupt can be adjusted to a predetermined probability based on a player’s bankroll. The Kelly bet value k is calculated “by multiplying the percent chance to win by two, then subtracting one-hundred percent.” [1]


k = P * 2 - 100%, where P = the player’s percent chance of winning in the long run. (P ≈ 50.5% at a true +1)


If players were to apply these ideas to Blackjack, then assuming they play using Liberal Vegas Strip rules where the player has a 0.28% disadvantage [2] to the house, then at a true count of +1, the player will have about a 0.72% advantage, then a 1.72% advantage at a true +2, then a 2.72%, and so on. This change of 1% in the advantage of the player per positive true count leads to the idea behind bet spreads based on Kelly bets.


For example, assuming a player has a true count of a +1, then their odds of winning are about 50.72% in the long run. This means that the k value at a true count of +1 for Liberal Vegas Strip rules is 1.44% (50.72% * 2 - 100%). The k value at a true +2 is 3.44%, at a true +3 the k value is 5.44% and so forth. These are the values of a full Kelly bet. These percentages should be multiplied by the total bankroll of a player to know how much to bet to reach the maximal expected value. [1] However, betting at a full value k can be risky in the long run. A player betting at a full Kelly value will have about a 13.5% chance of going bankrupt.[3]


A Kelly fractional bet is when a Kelly bettor bets at a fraction of their Kelly bet. If a bettor bets at one full Kelly, then the value of k = 1. If a Kelly bettor bets at one fourth Kelly, then k = 0.25. Figure 52 gives the probability of going bankrupt given a Kelly bettor bets at a Kelly value k multiplied by their current bankroll X.

Kelly betting is how most, if not all, professional card counting teams approach their betting behavior. Many card counting teams will keep their betting units at about 0.3-0.2k in order to keep their risk of ruin well below 1%. [3] The idea behind Kelly fractional betting is to value not going bankrupt over maximal profit gain. Using a Kelly value of 1 leads to maximal expected value [1]; however, as we’ve previously shown, this would incur a 13.53% risk of losing everything. Most card counting teams who have a significantly large bankroll do not believe a risk of that size is worth maximal value, so they will typically take less profit in exchange for a significantly low risk of ruin. Kelly fractional bettors might not make maximal expected value in the long run, but they will still make a significant profit over time while being almost guaranteed to not go bankrupt. [3]
